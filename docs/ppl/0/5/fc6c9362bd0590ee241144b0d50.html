MARCH, Abraham Wolf, radiologist, was born in East Chicago, Ind., Dec. 11, 1909, son of Wolf and Buna (Hershkovich) Marcovich, who came to this country from Romania about 1903 and settled in Indiana. His father was a businessman. Abraham W March legally changed his surname in 1956. He re- ceived his preliminary education at public schools in his native community and was graduated B.S. in 1932, M.C. in 1936, and M.D. in 1937 at the Uni- versity of Chicago. From the last-named year to 1939 he was an assistant resident in roentgenology at the University of Chicago clinics and during 1939-41 served as an assistant in roentgenology in the depart- ment of medicine at the university. After service as a consultant in radiology to the Kellogg Foundation during 1941-43, he was assistant professor of radiol- ogy at the University of Chicago for a year. In 1944 he entered the private practice of radiology in Dayton,
Ohio, where he continued until his retirement in 1967. He was on the consulting staff of the Miami Valley Hospital after 1948 and of the Kettering Hos- pital after 1964, both in Dayton. The Kellogg Foun- dation, during his service with it, instituted a pilot program to test the feasibility of bringing urban medical expertise to suburban and rural areas at greatly reduced cost. March established X-ray de- partments in five rural clinics, supervised the pur- chase of equipment, and trained X-ray technicians for these departments. He then served the five clinics as consulting radiologist, also training physicians to interpret X-ray films. The Kellogg Foundation pro- gram was considered highly successful. Throughout is career March engaged in extensive research; emphasizing diabetes mellitus. In 1956 the Charles F. Kettering Foundation, Yellow Springs, Ohio, sponsored March's research program to determine a possible primary etiological agent in the malignant irreversible form of human diabetes mellitus. March developed and carried out this program jointly with his wife. In experiments conducted as part of the program, the separation of the insulin fraction from blood was effected by means of continuous electro- phoresis. In addition, the physiological effect of lowering the blood-sugar level by injecting the iso- lated blood fraction in rabbits was demonstrated. In 1964, under sponsorship of the Dayton Foundation, March and his wife conducted research at the Miami Valley Hospital Research Institute into the regenera- tion of pancreatic beta cells in alloxan-induced diabetes by prolonged and acute administration of 6-mercaptopurine. The following year, at the Uni- versity of Dayton, they sponsored their own research program, synthesizing a compound, an iodinated metabolite, that was capable of concentrating in the ancreas and visualiza on of that ingen for diagnostic purposes In 1969 March formulated a theory of the etiology of diabetes mellitus, in which he held that an un- natural metabolite entered the nucleus of the cell to alter the messenger-RNA so that the amino acid pro- duced was faulty or in the wrong sequence, and when programmed to produce insulin, formed a biolog- ically inactive type. Diabetes mellitus, according to March's theory, was caused by this metabolic defect. March was interested also in the work of the Diabetic Association of Dayton, an organization established primarily to disseminate information to diabetics. and he lectured before industrial groups on the im- portance of early discovery of diabetes. He was the author of a number of articles, including "Duodenal Ulcer-The Value of the Roentgenologic Demon- stration of Crater" (Jour. AMA, Nov. 1938), "The Immediate and Late Effects of Intrathecal Injection
of Lipiodol" (ibid., May 1941), "The Myelographic Diagnosis of Intramedullary Spinal Cord Tumors" (Amer. Jour. Roentgenol., Mar. 1941),

March was a diplo- nate of the American Board of Radiology, a fellov t the American College ot Radiology, and a membe of the American Roentgen Ray Society, Radiological Society of North America, Ohio State and Miami Valley radiological societies, American Medical As- sociation, Ohio State and Montgomery County med- ical societies, American Diabetes Association, and the Town, YMCA Health, and Discussion clubs of Dayton. His religious affiliation was with the Jewish faith, and he was a member of Temple Israel, Day- ton. Politically he was an independent. In his leisure time he enjoyed traveling, swimming, and playing golf and tennis.
